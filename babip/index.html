A Lot of Baseball is Luck

How One Analyst Accidentally Upended Baseball Statitistics

By Tim Marco

Late in the last millennium, Voros McCracken realized something that completely revolutionized how people thought about baseball statistics. Sportswriter Jeff Passan has said that his discovery is among "the most seminal theories" developed by baseball's then newly-influential group of armchair analysts.

As it turns out, his idea is also a great introduction into sabermetrics (the term for statistical analysis in baseball).

Voros had noticed something peculiar about pitching. He was looking at the rate that balls hit into play—that is, every pitch that wasn't a strikeout, walk or homerun—wound up as hits. This rate is now known as BABIP, or batting average on balls in play, and it's very influential in baseball analysis.

McCracken pulled together some data on the BABIP for pitchers of the time, and saw patterns like the ones below for Greg Maddux, Pedro Martinez, and Randy Johnson.

Do you see the pattern there? If you do, you're likely falling victim to apophenia, or the tendency to see patterns in randomness.

The randomness inherent in these numbers hinted at a very surprising idea: pitchers had no control over what happened to balls hit into play.

Of course, looking at the career of just three pitchers wouldn't past muster. They could be outliers. If you want to know if patterns really exist, you have to look at a lot of data.

So Voros did just that. He started looking at the BABIP for pitchers year-over-year, to see how well they were correlated.

In particular, he measured the r2 (pronounced "r-squared") values from one season to the next.

This value is just a simple way of seeing how well one data point predicts another. If the values were closely correlated – if a pitcher had some ability to cause a low BABIP – the r2 value would be close to 1. If it was all just luck, the value would be closer to zero.

And when you run the numbers, it turns out that there is actually very little correlation. Between 2014 and 2013, for example, the value for all qualified pitchers in MLB was 0.21.

In other words, whether a ball in play is a hit or an out is pretty much a crap shoot. One year, those balls may be harmless popups. But the next year, they may drop in for bad-luck singles.

Whichever happens is pure luck. Pitchers can't control it.

This was a huge surprise. Nobody believed it. Even Voros didn't believe it. But the numbers were clear.
