How Autonomous Trucking Can Impact Drivers

By Tim Marco

One of the most potentially disruptive technologies today is the self-driving automobile. Over the past few years, many companies have been testing vehicles that are autonomous to varying degrees.

Although Google's cutesy robotic passenger vehicles tend to get the most attention in the media, there are good reasons to believe that the first wave of vehicle automation won't immediately affect typical drivers. Instead, long-haul commercial trucking seems perfectly poised for a rapid autonomous takeover.

The Economics of Long-Haul Trucking

The American Transportation Research Institute released a fascinating study in 2014 on the per-mile costs of the long-haul trucking. We can use this information to start to evaluate the incentives that trucking operators might face in the future.

According to the study, there are two major sources of costs for trucking: vehicle costs and driver costs.

Vehicle Costs vs. Driver Costs

The costs associated with a human driver are a major portion of the total costs of trucking. And regardless of one's own feelings on whether this should be the case, it's very clear that operators will remove these costs if they can.

There's a huge incentive to do so.

Impact by State

While these efficiencies will no doubt be good for trucking companies, and will likely mean some long-term savings for consumers, their largest immediate impacts will be on the drivers and the communities they live in.

Long-haul trucking alone is a major industry in the US. And as a result, drivers constitute a not-insignificant portion of the tax base in many places.
