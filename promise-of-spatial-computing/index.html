[Promise of Spatial Computing]

Intro

  • Define what I'm talking about in terms of Spatial Computing / Mixed Reality
  • Onion's concept of Glowing Rectangles as metaphor for what computing is today.
  • Mixed Reality / Spatial Computing provides a mechanism to get beyond that.
  • But what that really means isn't that we'll have better computers. It's that we'll have different computers, that do different things.
  • The whole point is to help people accomplish tasks that are intrinsically spatial.
  • Allude to (don't explain technical challenges)
  • Effect change, Learn from objects, Learn how to manipulate objects

What Not To Do

  • One common use case / idea is basically a floating glowing rectangle
  • [Mockup of glowing rectangles]
  • This strikes me as silly. It makes no sense. Why would you not just use a computer? Consider all of the additional cost, complexity, and overhead.
  • The problem here is that nothing here is intrinsically spatial, which is the whole point of MR / Spatial Computing
  • Charts? Virtual Characters? Etc.

Effect Changes

  • [Video of Lights]
  • Norman's DOET devotes a chapter to designing light switches
  • His point was that one of the fundamental challenges of design is establishing mappings between the abstract real and controls for physical devices.
  • That's the primary promise of spatial computing. In an ideal world, the interface just disappears.
  • Controlling an individual light isn't that big. But imagine controlling any object.

Learn how to manipulate objects and move in space

  • [Video of Keurig]
  • We can't always control objects directly through computers. We don't necessarily always want to control objects
  • Sometimes, we just need to know how to manipulate, change, or navigate objects in space.
  • Compare this to using manuals or maps. As above, the benefit is that the information is there: contextually and spatially relevant.

Learn from objects

  • [Big Hurt Sculpture]
  • Sometimes, we just want to learn from an object.
  • We want to draw attention to details or parts, or tell a story about an object, as in a museum.
  • But the key is from an object. This doesn't really work all that well when it's just a 3D display of a virtual object.

Conclusion

  • Spatial computing could (in theory) represent a new paradigm for computing
  • But that's because it's different, not because it's better at what we aleady do.
  • So we need to follow these principles for design.
  • But there are real limitations to the technology. HMDs basically suck. Phones are literal glowing rectangles. So until those are solved, this is in part just academic.
